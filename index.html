Financial Statement Analysis

Reading the health of a firm

The toolkit for interpreting a company's financial condition — ratio analysis across leverage, liquidity, efficiency, profitability, and market value. Financial analysis evaluates the viability, stability, and profitability of a business by examining the relationships between different pieces of financial information, over time and against industry benchmarks.

1. Methods of Financial Analysis

Financial analysis can be achieved through three principal methods: ratio analysis (the most common), valuation through cash flows and comparables, and competitive benchmarking. Ratio analysis allows comparison over time (time-series) and across firms (cross-sectional), with measurements expressed as percentages, multiples, or number of days.

2. Leverage Ratios

Leverage ratios address a firm's long-term ability to meet its obligations and the degree of financial leverage employed.

Debt Ratio
$\dfrac{\text{Long-term Debt}}{\text{Long-term Debt} + \text{Equity}}$
Proportion of total capitalisation funded by debt. Higher = more leveraged.
Debt-Equity Ratio
$\dfrac{\text{Long-term Debt}}{\text{Equity}}$
Direct comparison of debt to equity. Can be expressed as ×, %, or as a ratio.

3. Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios refer to a firm's ability to satisfy short-term obligations without undue stress.

Current Ratio
$\dfrac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}$
Broad measure of short-term solvency. A ratio above 1.0 suggests adequate coverage.
Quick Ratio
$\dfrac{\text{Current Assets} - \text{Inventory}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}$
Conservative view — excludes inventory, which may be illiquid.

4. Efficiency Ratios

Efficiency ratios measure how intensively a firm uses its assets to generate revenue.

Asset Turnover
$\dfrac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Total Assets}}$
Revenue generated per unit of assets. Fast-food: ~2.0×; Luxury hotel: ~0.5×.
Inventory Turnover
$\dfrac{\text{COGS}}{\text{Inventory}}$
How quickly inventory is sold and replaced within a period.
Receivables Turnover
$\dfrac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Accounts Receivable}}$
Speed at which the firm collects cash from customers.

5. Profitability Ratios

Profit Margin
$\dfrac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Sales}}$
How much of each £ of revenue becomes profit.
Return on Assets
$\text{ROA} = \dfrac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Total Assets}}$
Profit generated per unit of assets employed.
Return on Equity
$\text{ROE} = \dfrac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}}$
Return earned on the capital shareholders have invested.

6. Market-to-Value Ratios

Price-Earnings Ratio
$\text{P/E} = \dfrac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{EPS}}$
How much investors are willing to pay per £1 of earnings.
Market-to-Book
$\dfrac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Book Value per Share}}$
Premium (or discount) the market assigns relative to accounting value.

7. DuPont Analysis

The DuPont system links profitability and efficiency ratios to decompose returns into their underlying drivers.

DuPont — Return on Assets
$$\text{ROA} = \underbrace{\frac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Total Assets}}}_{\text{Asset Turnover}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Sales}}}_{\text{Profit Margin}}$$
DuPont — Return on Equity (3-part)
$$\text{ROE} = \underbrace{\frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Sales}}}_{\text{Margin}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Assets}}}_{\text{Turnover}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Assets}}{\text{Equity}}}_{\text{Leverage}}$$
Worked Example
Two Business Models, Same ROA

Both businesses achieve 10% ROA, but through entirely different strategies. DuPont reveals that the fast-food chain relies on high volume (turnover) while the hotel relies on high margin.

Subject II

Foundations of Corporate Finance

The architecture of financial decision-making

Corporate finance centres on three fundamental questions that every financial manager must answer: in what long-lived assets should the firm invest? How should it raise the cash to fund those investments? And how should it manage day-to-day operating cash flows? These questions define the discipline and shape the value of the firm.

The Three Pillars

Financial Markets & the Firm

Financial markets serve as the intermediary between the firm and its investors. The firm issues securities (debt and equity) to raise capital, invests in assets, generates cash flows, pays taxes, and distributes the remainder as dividends and debt repayments — or retains them for future investment.

Figure 2.1 — The circular flow of cash between firms, financial markets, and government

The Agency Problem

The corporate form separates ownership (shareholders) from control (managers). This separation enables professional management and facilitates large-scale enterprise, but introduces the principal-agent problem: managers may pursue objectives that diverge from shareholders' interests.

The Goal of the Firm

From the shareholder's viewpoint, the financial manager has one overriding objective: maximise the value of each share. This is distinct from profit maximisation — value maximisation accounts for the timing, magnitude, and risk of future cash flows, not merely accounting earnings in a single period.

Subject III

Time Value of Money & Investment Decisions

The mechanics of value creation

A pound today is worth more than a pound tomorrow. This deceptively simple insight — the first principle of corporate finance — underpins every valuation, every investment decision, and every financing choice. Because money can be invested to earn a return immediately, cash flows occurring at different points in time cannot be compared directly.

First Principle of Corporate Finance
A pound today is worth more than a pound tomorrow

Future Value & Present Value

Future Value
$$FV = C \times (1 + r)^t$$
Present Value
$$PV = \frac{C}{(1 + r)^t}$$

where $C$ is the cash flow, $r$ is the interest (discount) rate, and $t$ is the number of periods.

Worked Example
Compound Interest over 2 Years

Deposit £100 at 10% compound interest for 2 years:

$FV = £100 \times (1.10)^2 = £121$

Conversely, the present value of £121 received in 2 years at 10% is:

$PV = \dfrac{£121}{(1.10)^2} = £100$

Risk & the Discount Rate

Second Principle of Corporate Finance
A safe pound is worth more than a risky pound

Higher risk demands a higher discount rate, reducing present value.

Required Return
$$r_{\text{investment}} = r_{\text{risk-free}} + \text{risk premium}$$
Worked Example
Impact of Risk on PV

A machine will produce £500,000 next year. Cost: £450,000.

Discount RatePVDecision
5% (low risk)£476,190Accept ✓
11% (moderate risk)£450,450Accept ✓ (barely)
14% (high risk)£438,596Reject ✗

Net Present Value

Net Present Value
$$NPV = -C_0 + \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{C_t}{(1+r)^t}$$
Decision Rule

NPV > 0 → Accept (the project creates value). NPV < 0 → Reject (the project destroys value). NPV is additive: NPV(A+B) = NPV(A) + NPV(B).

Perpetuities & Annuities

Perpetuity
$PV = \dfrac{C}{r}$
Constant payment forever
Growing Perpetuity
$PV = \dfrac{C}{r - g}$
Payment growing at rate g forever
Annuity
$PV = C \times \dfrac{1 - (1+r)^{-t}}{r}$
Level payment for t periods

IRR, Payback & Other Criteria

Internal Rate of Return
$$0 = -C_0 + \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{C_t}{(1+IRR)^t}$$

Rule: Accept if $IRR > r$ (cost of capital). But beware: multiple IRRs with non-conventional cash flows, incorrect rankings for mutually exclusive projects, and cases where IRR simply cannot be computed.

Figure 3.1 — NPV profile: as the discount rate rises, NPV falls. IRR is where the curve crosses zero.

Valuation of Securities

Pricing equity and debt

Every financial instrument — whether a share of equity or a bond — derives its value from the cash flows it will deliver to its holder, discounted to the present. The mechanics differ between equity and debt, but the principle is identical: price equals the present value of all expected future cash flows.

Share Valuation — Dividend Discount Model

General Share Pricing
$$P_0 = \frac{D_1 + P_1}{(1+r)}$$
Gordon Growth Model
$$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r - g}$$
Worked Example
Non-Constant Growth Valuation

A company pays dividends of $1.30, $1.80, $2.00 in years 1–3. Beyond year 3, dividends grow at 3% forever. Discount rate is 7%.

Year123Beyond 3
Dividends$1.30$1.80$2.00$2.06 (growing at 3%)
Share price at yr 3——$P_3 = \frac{2.06}{0.07 - 0.03} = \$51.50$

Discounting all cash flows: $P_0 = \frac{1.30}{1.07} + \frac{1.80}{1.07^2} + \frac{2.00 + 51.50}{1.07^3} = \$46.46$

Determinants of r and g

Required Return Decomposition
$$r = \underbrace{\frac{D_1}{P_0}}_{\text{Dividend Yield}} + \underbrace{g}_{\text{Capital Gain Yield}}$$
Sustainable Growth Rate
$$g = b \times ROE$$

where $b$ is the plowback (retention) ratio — the fraction of earnings reinvested rather than paid out as dividends.

Bond Valuation

Bond Price
$$P = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{C}{(1+YTM)^t} + \frac{F}{(1+YTM)^T}$$
Worked Example
LVMH Bond Pricing

Maturity: 3 years. Face value: €1,000. Coupon rate: 15%. YTM: 6%.

$P = \frac{150}{1.06} + \frac{150}{1.06^2} + \frac{150 + 1{,}000}{1.06^3} = €1{,}240.57$

Since YTM (6%) < coupon rate (15%), the bond trades at a premium.

Bond Pricing Rules

Premium: YTM < Coupon Rate → Price > Face Value
At par: YTM = Coupon Rate → Price = Face Value
Discount: YTM > Coupon Rate → Price < Face Value

Interest Rate Risk

Bond prices and interest rates move inversely. Longer maturity and lower coupons amplify sensitivity.

Subject V

Risk, Return & Portfolio Theory

The science of diversification, CAPM, and beta

Risk and return always go together. The central insight of modern portfolio theory is that investors can reduce risk — without proportionally sacrificing return — by holding diversified portfolios. But not all risk can be diversified away. The residual, systematic risk, measured by beta, is the only risk the market compensates. The Capital Asset Pricing Model formalises this relationship and underpins the estimation of the cost of equity — a critical input to WACC and company valuation.

1. Measuring Risk

Risk is defined as the chance of the actual result being different from the expected result. In finance, risk is quantified by how far actual returns deviate from expected returns. The standard measures are variance and standard deviation — the higher they are, the greater the risk.

Variance & Standard Deviation
$$\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(R_i - \bar{R})^2 \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$$
Deviations are squared to prevent positive and negative values from cancelling out.

2. Portfolio Risk & Diversification

While portfolio return is simply the weighted average of individual asset returns, portfolio risk depends critically on how assets move together — measured by covariance and correlation. When correlation is below +1, combining assets reduces portfolio risk below the weighted average of individual risks. This is the power of diversification.

Two-Asset Portfolio Variance
$$\sigma_p^2 = a^2\sigma_X^2 + b^2\sigma_Y^2 + 2ab\,\rho_{XY}\,\sigma_X\sigma_Y$$
When ρ < 1, the cross-term shrinks, pulling portfolio risk below the weighted average.
Worked Example
Diversification in Action
PortfolioE(Return)Std Dev (σ)
100% Share X10.0%8.72%
50% X / 50% Y9.0%4.97%
100% Share Y8.0%8.41%

The 50/50 portfolio has a lower risk than either individual share while earning a return between the two. The return fell by only 1 percentage point but risk fell by nearly half.

3. Systematic vs. Non-Systematic Risk

As more securities are added to a portfolio, risk continues to decline — but only up to a point. The risk that can be eliminated by diversification is called non-systematic risk (also firm-specific, diversifiable, or unique risk). The risk that cannot be eliminated is called systematic risk (also market risk or non-diversifiable risk). Total risk = systematic + non-systematic.

Figure 5.1 — As securities are added, non-systematic risk is diversified away. Systematic risk remains.
Critical Consequence

Since non-systematic risk can be diversified away for free, investors are not compensated for bearing it. The market only rewards systematic risk. This is why we need a measure of systematic risk — and that measure is beta (β).

4. Beta (β) — Definition, Regression & Interpretation

Beta is the specific measure used for assessing the level of systematic risk. It measures the sensitivity of a security's returns to the fluctuations of the overall market. Formally, beta is derived by regressing the security's historical returns against the market's returns. The slope of the regression line is the beta.

Beta — Regression Definition
$$r_i^o = \alpha_i + \beta_i \cdot r_m + \varepsilon_i$$
The observed return on security i = alpha (intercept) + beta × market return + error term. The slope β captures the systematic component.
Beta — Covariance/Variance Formula
$$\beta_i = \frac{\text{Cov}(r_i,\, r_m)}{\text{Var}(r_m)} = \frac{\sigma_{i,m}}{\sigma_m^2}$$
Beta = systematic risk of security i ÷ total risk of the market portfolio.
Figure 5.2 — Beta is the slope of the regression of security returns against market returns

Portfolio Beta

The beta of a portfolio is the weighted average of the betas of its individual securities: $\beta_p = \sum w_i \beta_i$. This means you can engineer any desired level of systematic risk by adjusting portfolio weights.

5. The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)

Developed by Sharpe, Treynor, and Lintner in the 1960s, CAPM provides the framework for determining the expected return on any asset based solely on its systematic risk. The model rests on three assumptions: (1) investors can trade without taxes or transaction costs and borrow/lend at the risk-free rate; (2) all investors hold efficient portfolios; (3) all investors have homogeneous expectations about returns, variances, and covariances.

The CAPM Equation
$$E(r_i) = \underbrace{r_f}_{\text{Risk-free rate}} + \underbrace{\beta_i}_{\text{Systematic risk}} \times \underbrace{\left(E(r_m) - r_f\right)}_{\text{Market risk premium}}$$
Expected return = compensation for time (rƒ) + compensation for systematic risk (β × market risk premium).

The CAPM says that the risk premium on a particular asset is proportional to the market risk premium, scaled by the asset's beta. A stock with β = 1.5 should earn 1.5 times the market risk premium above the risk-free rate. A stock with β = 0.7 earns only 0.7 times the market risk premium.

Worked Example
CAPM — Expected Return Calculation

Risk-free rate = 3%. Market risk premium = 8%. What is the expected return for:

CompanyβCalculationE(rᵢ)
Company A1.503% + 1.50 × 8%15.0%
Firm Z0.703% + 0.70 × 8%8.6%
Market portfolio1.003% + 1.00 × 8%11.0%

Higher beta → higher expected return. The market itself always sits at β = 1.

6. The Security Market Line (SML) & Alpha

The SML is the graphical representation of CAPM. It plots expected return on the vertical axis against beta on the horizontal axis. The line passes through two anchor points: the risk-free rate (β = 0) and the market portfolio (β = 1). Every fairly priced security should lie exactly on the SML.

Figure 5.3 — The Security Market Line: expected return vs. beta. Fairly priced assets lie on the line.

Alpha (α) is the difference between a security's actual expected return and the return predicted by CAPM. Securities with positive alpha sit above the SML — they are underpriced and offer excess return. Investors will buy them, pushing prices up and returns down until they fall back onto the SML. Securities with negative alpha sit below the SML — overpriced. Investors sell them, pushing prices down and returns up.

8. Drivers of Beta — Operating & Financial Leverage

A company's beta reflects two sources of risk: business risk (from operations) and financial risk (from leverage). Both amplify sensitivity to the economic cycle.

Operating Leverage

Companies with a high ratio of fixed costs to total costs have high operating leverage. When revenues rise, profits rise faster (because fixed costs don't increase) — but when revenues fall, profits fall faster too. This makes earnings more volatile, pushing beta higher. Capital-intensive industries (cement, airlines, steel) tend to have high operating leverage and therefore higher betas. Service retailers with mostly variable costs have lower operating leverage and lower betas.

Financial Leverage

Firms with debt must make interest payments regardless of their sales. These fixed financing costs further amplify the volatility of returns to equity holders. The greater the debt-to-equity ratio, the higher the equity beta.

Figure 5.5 — As the debt-to-equity ratio increases, equity beta rises above the asset (unlevered) beta

9. Levered vs. Unlevered Beta

A company's observed (equity) beta incorporates both business risk and financial risk from leverage. When comparing firms with different capital structures, or when a firm changes its own capital structure, we need to separate these two components. This is done by unlevering and relevering beta.

Unlevering Beta — Remove Financial Risk
$$\beta_{\text{Asset}} = \frac{\beta_{\text{Equity}}}{1 + \dfrac{D}{E}\,(1-T)}$$
The asset (unlevered) beta measures only the business risk of the firm's operations, ignoring leverage.
Relevering Beta — Apply New Capital Structure
$$\beta_{\text{Equity}} = \beta_{\text{Asset}} \times \left[1 + \frac{D}{E}\,(1-T)\right]$$
Same formula rearranged. Apply the asset beta to a new D/E ratio to get the new equity beta.
Figure 5.6 — Two-step process: unlever a peer's beta to isolate business risk, then relever with your own D/E ratio
Worked Example
Unlevering & Relevering — Acura Technology enters Biotech

Setup: Acura Technology wants to enter the biotech industry. Biogene, a biotech peer, has equity β = 0.90, with $95m equity and $5m debt. Acura intends a 60% equity-to-value ratio (i.e. 40% debt). No tax.

Step 1 — Unlever Biogene's beta:

$\beta_{\text{Asset}} = \frac{0.90}{1 + \frac{5}{95}} = \frac{0.90}{1.053} = 0.855$

Step 2 — Relever with Acura's target D/E: D/E = 40%/60% = 0.667

$\beta_{\text{Equity}} = 0.855 \times \left(1 + 0.667\right) = 0.855 \times 1.667 = 1.425$

Result: Acura should use β = 1.425 for the new biotech division. The higher beta reflects the additional financial risk from Acura's more leveraged capital structure, even though the underlying business risk (βAsset = 0.855) is the same as Biogene's.

10. From CAPM to Cost of Equity to WACC

The entire chain of analysis connects as follows: CAPM determines the cost of equity ($r_E$), which feeds into the WACC, which serves as the discount rate for DCF valuation. This is why getting beta right — and understanding when to lever or unlever it — matters enormously for company valuation.

Figure 5.7 — The analytical chain: CAPM → Cost of Equity → WACC → DCF Valuation
WACC — Where it All Comes Together
$$WACC = \frac{E}{D+E} \times \underbrace{r_E}_{\text{From CAPM}} + \frac{D}{D+E} \times r_D \times (1-T)$$
WACC is the weighted average of the CAPM-derived cost of equity and the after-tax cost of debt.
Why This Matters

A small error in beta cascades through the entire valuation. If you overestimate β by 0.2, the cost of equity rises by ~1.2% (assuming 6% market risk premium), which can shift the enterprise value by 10–15% or more. This is why analysts spend significant time estimating beta carefully, selecting appropriate peers, and deciding whether to lever or unlever.

Subject VI

Company Valuation & Cost of Capital

What is a firm worth?

Valuation is not an exact science — there is no single correct value for a company, only a range of defensible estimates. The analyst's task is to combine DCF analysis with multiples-based methods, stress-test assumptions through sensitivity analysis, and arrive at a valuation range that can anchor negotiations and investment decisions.

Weighted Average Cost of Capital

WACC
$$WACC = \frac{E}{D+E} \times r_E + \frac{D}{D+E} \times r_D \times (1-T)$$
Worked Example
Calculating WACC

Market value of equity = €150m. Market value of debt = €100m. Cost of equity = 14.6%. Pre-tax cost of debt = 8.0%. Tax rate = 35%.

$WACC = \frac{150}{250} \times 14.6\% + \frac{100}{250} \times 8\% \times (1-0.35) = 8.76\% + 2.08\% = 10.84\%$

Subject VII

Capital Structure & Derivatives

Financing decisions and risk management

How a firm finances itself — the mix of debt and equity in its capital structure — is one of the most consequential decisions in corporate finance. Modigliani and Miller demonstrated that in a perfect world, capital structure is irrelevant. But taxes, bankruptcy costs, and information asymmetries make it anything but.

Modigliani-Miller Propositions

MM Proposition 1 (No Taxes)
$$V_L = V_U$$
The value of a levered firm equals the value of an unlevered firm. Capital structure is irrelevant.
MM Proposition 2 (No Taxes)
$$r_E = r_A + \frac{D}{E}(r_A - r_D)$$
Cost of equity rises linearly with leverage. WACC remains constant.
Figure 7.1 — As leverage increases, cost of equity rises but WACC remains constant (no taxes)

Effect of Taxes

MM with Taxes
$$V_L = V_U + \tau \times D$$
The value of the levered firm equals the unlevered value plus the PV of the tax shield.
Interest Tax Shield

Interest tax shield = Interest payments × Corporate tax rate. Since interest is tax-deductible, debt creates a "shield" that reduces the firm's tax bill, increasing total cash flows to investors.

Trade-Off vs. Pecking-Order Theory

Options — Payoff Diagrams

Option Payoffs

Put-Call Parity
$$S + P = \frac{X}{(1+r_f)^t} + C$$
Holding a share + put must cost the same as holding a bond + call (same strike, same expiration).
